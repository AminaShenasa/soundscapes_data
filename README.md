# Soundscapes Data

This is the GitHub repository for a Master's Thesis by Amina Shenasa. The purpose of this project is to integrate ecological theory, soundscape ecology, and machine learning to address a key gap in ecosystem monitoring: the detection of dynamic but critical ecological transitions.

In this study, I tested whether soundscapes can serve as reliable indicators of kelp
forest ecosystem conditions in Sitka Sound, Alaska. Specifically, I addressed whether (1)
acoustics can distinguish between kelp forest and urchin barren habitats, (2) whether this
distinction can detect varying levels of habitat type (whole community structures across
sites and individual species densities), and (3) whether the sounds discriminating habitat
types are made up at least in part by sea urchin activity acoustics, and whether such
acoustics allow for assessment of sea urchin and M. pyrifera stipe densities. To address
these questions, I conducted paired acoustic and ecological surveys at giant kelp forests,
Macrocystis pyrifera, and urchin barren habitats. Additionally, I recorded sea urchin activity acoustics to understand their role in the broader soundscape and potential patterns
of activity

By evaluating how traditional ecological indicators can be predicted by acoustic metrics in assessing kelp forest health, and how these ecological variables interrelate and manifest acoustically, this study explores how integrating soundscape data with traditional monitoring may enhance our ability to detect and respond to ecological change, ultimately improving ecosystem management and conservation efforts.

Thesis Title: Beyond the Visual: Detecting Ecosystem Transitions Through Soundscapes

Abstract:
Understanding how ecosystem states shift and identifying early signs of those
transitions are critical for effective conservation. In kelp forest ecosystems, the collapse
into urchin barrens represents a well-documented phase shift, yet transitional states and
their early detection remain poorly understood. Traditional monitoring methods, such as
SCUBA surveys with periodic ecological assessments, provide only snapshots of
dynamic systems, often missing rapid and punctuated changes that define these transition
states. Given the acoustic complexity of kelp forests, soundscapes may provide a
continuous method to determine sensitive indicators of ecological transitions that visual
surveys may miss. This study evaluates whether soundscapes can serve as reliable
indicators of kelp forest ecosystem conditions in Sitka Sound, Alaska. Surveying three
Macrocystis pyrifera forest habitats and three urchin barren habitats revealed that
soundscapes can distinguish between binary state conditions (Macrocystis forest vs
urchin barren), and importantly, can also distinguish between a range of ecological
conditions. Additionally, in situ recordings of red sea urchin acoustics were found to be,
at least in part, driving the soundscapes of Macrocystis forest ecosystems. These results
suggest that soundscapes can capture both distinct ecosystem states and intermediate
conditions, offering scalable, non-invasive methods for early detection of ecological

change. Overall, this work highlights the potential of passive acoustics to complement
traditional surveys and improve the detection of ecosystem transitions in marine
environments.
